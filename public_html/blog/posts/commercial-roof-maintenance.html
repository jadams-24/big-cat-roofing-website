Why Michigan's Climate Demands Bi-Annual Inspections

Spring Inspection (April-May): Winter Damage Assessment

Michigan winters are brutal on commercial roofs. Spring inspections identify and address:

Freeze-Thaw Damage

  • Membrane splits: Temperature cycling causes material fatigue
  • Fastener backing out: Expansion/contraction loosens attachments
  • Flashing separation: Ice formation breaks adhesive bonds
  • Ponding from snow melt: Identifies drainage issues
  • HVAC curb damage: Ice accumulation stresses penetrations

Structural Concerns

  • Deck deflection from snow loads
  • Insulation compression reducing R-value
  • Interior leak evidence from winter
  • Gutter and downspout damage

Fall Inspection (September-October): Winter Preparation

Fall inspections prepare your roof for Michigan's harsh winter:

Preventive Measures

  • Drainage clearing: Ensure proper water flow
  • Sealant renewal: Address aging caulks before freezing
  • Debris removal: Prevent drain blockages
  • Insulation verification: Confirm thermal protection
  • Snow load preparation: Identify vulnerable areas

Comprehensive Inspection Components

1. Membrane Evaluation

The roof membrane is your primary defense against water infiltration:

Visual Inspection Points

  • Surface condition: Cracking, blistering, or alligatoring
  • Seam integrity: Check all field and perimeter seams
  • Puncture identification: From foot traffic or debris
  • UV degradation: Especially on south-facing sections
  • Attachment verification: Ensure proper adhesion/fastening

Moisture Detection

  • Infrared scanning for hidden moisture
  • Core sampling in suspect areas
  • Electronic leak detection (ELD) testing
  • Nuclear moisture surveys for large roofs

2. Drainage System Analysis

Poor drainage causes 80% of premature commercial roof failures:

Critical Inspection Areas

  • Primary drains: Clear of debris, proper flow rate
  • Overflow scuppers: Unobstructed and functional
  • Gutters and leaders: Secure attachment, no leaks
  • Ponding water: Document location and depth
  • Tapered insulation: Verify positive drainage

3. Flashing and Edge Details

Flashings are the most vulnerable points on any commercial roof:

Inspection Checklist

  • Base flashings: Secure attachment, proper height
  • Counter flashings: Sealed and mechanically fastened
  • Penetration flashings: All HVAC, plumbing, electrical
  • Expansion joints: Flexible and watertight
  • Parapet walls: Coping secure, mortar intact

4. Rooftop Equipment Assessment

HVAC and other equipment create penetrations and traffic patterns:

Equipment Inspection Points

  • Curb integrity and pitch pans
  • Condensate line drainage
  • Support block distribution
  • Access path wear patterns
  • Equipment screen attachments

Types of Commercial Roofing Systems

EPDM (Rubber) Roofing

Popular for Metro Detroit flat roofs due to cold weather performance:

Maintenance Focus Areas

  • Seam inspection: Adhesive degradation over time
  • Membrane shrinkage: Common in older systems
  • Puncture prevention: From rooftop traffic
  • Flashing attachment: Adhesive vs. mechanical

TPO (Thermoplastic) Roofing

Energy-efficient option gaining popularity in Michigan:

Specific Maintenance Needs

  • Weld inspection: Heat-welded seams can fail
  • UV protection: White surface degrades over time
  • Expansion/contraction: Monitor for stress cracking
  • Chemical resistance: Check around kitchen exhausts

Modified Bitumen

Traditional choice for many Warren and Sterling Heights buildings:

Maintenance Priorities

  • Granule loss: Indicates UV damage
  • Blister formation: From moisture intrusion
  • Lap seal integrity: Torch-applied or cold adhesive
  • Coating renewal: Extends life significantly

Metal Roofing Systems

Standing seam and R-panel systems common on warehouses:

Metal-Specific Maintenance

  • Fastener inspection: Backing out over time
  • Rust prevention: Address immediately
  • Sealant renewal: At penetrations and end laps
  • Thermal movement: Monitor for oil canning
